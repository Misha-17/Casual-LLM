01

Pearl's ladder of causation

All causal reasoning sits on one of three rungs. The rung determines what kind of question is being asked and what information is needed to answer it correctly. Each level is strictly harder than the one below it.

Rung 1
Association
Observing correlations. What is P(Y|X)?
Rung 2
Intervention
Do-calculus. What happens if we set X = x?
Rung 3
Counterfactual
Abduction, action, and prediction in sequence.

A model that cannot reliably execute Rung 2 cannot do Rung 3. The benchmark literature tracks progress across all three, but the distribution of failure is highly uneven, and the hardest rung remains mostly unsolved for the right reasons.

02

Benchmark landscape

CLadder (NeurIPS 2023)

The primary causal reasoning benchmark. Over 10,000 questions spanning all three rungs, evaluated using CausalCoT: a six-step chain-of-thought protocol mirroring the formal causal inference engine. The six steps are graph extraction, query classification, data collection, symbolic formalization, estimand derivation, and arithmetic.

The leaderboard is saturated in-distribution. The benchmark is not solved. Under perturbation, performance collapses by 30 to 62 points across all models. Robustness is now the actual open problem.

Corr2Cause (ICLR 2024)

Tests causal discovery from correlations. Given correlational statements about abstract variables, can the model determine valid causal relationships using the PC algorithm? The headline finding is a robustness catastrophe.

A 62-point F1 collapse under variable renaming. The fine-tuned model learned which words correlate with which labels, not the causal rules. The exception is Llama-3-8B trained on abstract graph axioms (d-separation, transitivity, Markov conditions) using alphanumeric variable names. It scores 37 zero-shot, beating GPT-4 at 29, without ever seeing the task format. Axiomatic training generalises. Task-specific fine-tuning does not.

CauSciBench (2025)

The hardest and most realistic benchmark. 367 tasks from over 100 real research papers across nine scientific disciplines. The model receives a dataset and a question, and must autonomously select a method, implement it in Python, and interpret the result end-to-end.

77.2%
o3 + CoT

49%
Mean relative error
on actual estimates

That 77.2% sounds reasonable until you see that mean relative estimation error on the actual numbers is still 49%. Correct method selection and correct execution are different skills. Three systematic failures appear across every model tested.

OLS default bias
Models almost always reach for ordinary least squares regardless of whether it is appropriate, like a doctor who prescribes the same treatment for everything.
Method does not equal execution
Knowing the name of a technique and correctly running it are separate things. A model can select the right approach and still get the numbers badly wrong.
Confounder under-selection
ReAct-based agents optimise each action locally without stepping back to consider the full adjustment set. Sequential reasoning without global structure fails on confounder identification.
03

Post-training methods

Four post-training approaches have been applied to causal reasoning. Their relative gains are large, and the reason why matters as much as the numbers.

+1.4%
DPO
+6.4%
KTO
+19.2%
SFT
+42%
GRPO / PPO

GRPO and PPO dominate because they give the model corrective feedback on its own reasoning in real time. SFT teaches imitation of correct outputs. GRPO teaches how to think. DeepSeek-R1-14B with GRPO on CauGym reaches 93.5% on the CaLM benchmark, compared to OpenAI o3 at 55.4% without causal fine-tuning.

All four methods share the same out-of-distribution failure. In-distribution gains do not transfer to perturbed queries. This is the central unsolved problem and the motivation for the mechanistic work below.

Four findings from the literature are worth highlighting before moving on.

Entropy regularisation is load-bearing
Removing entropy regularisation from BinaryPPO collapses accuracy from 97% to 29%. The model without it learns to always predict the same label. This is not a hyperparameter choice; it is required for the method to work at all.
Axiomatic beats task-specific
Training on abstract causal graph rules with alphanumeric variables generalises to unseen task formats. Training on task examples does not. This is direct evidence that fine-tuned models learn lexical patterns, not causal structure.
SCM-GRPO prevents reward hacking
Standard GRPO lets models learn to generate long plausible-sounding chains that are logically wrong, because verbosity correlates with reward. SCM-GRPO grounds the reward in an actual Structural Causal Model, checking reasoning against the graph. Not yet combined with the other methods.
4-bit quantization hurts Rung 2
Compressed attention weights cannot fully sever pre-intervention correlations when simulating a do() operation, what the paper calls associative leakage. Injecting the ground-truth causal graph via GraphRAG recovers 1.7%. This matters for resource-constrained deployment.
04

Open problems

Five problems persist across the literature. The first two are critical. The others are important and connected.

Robustness gap
Every method that achieves high in-distribution accuracy collapses 30 to 62 points under simple variable renaming or paraphrase. No exception exists. The benchmark problem has moved from accuracy to robustness.
Abduction deficit
Rung 3 counterfactual reasoning requires three sub-steps: inferring hidden background variables from observations, applying the intervention, and computing the counterfactual outcome. Models fail at the first. Standard benchmarks including CLadder cheat by providing latent variable values directly. Real abduction is harder and largely unaddressed. SFT-trained models drop 25 to 40% when genuine abduction is required; RLVR achieves 1.5 to 2 times better accuracy on these tasks.
No process supervision
All current training uses outcome-level reward. No step-level process reward model exists for CausalCoT, despite clear behavioural evidence of which steps fail: S3 formalization sits at F1 42.1%, query classification at 50.7%.
Real-world estimation gap
Models trained on synthetic data do not transfer to real scientific datasets. Mean relative estimation error remains 49% on CauSciBench even at 77% method selection accuracy.
S3 encoding bottleneck
Symbolic formalization, translating a causal word problem into a formal expression, is weakly encoded in the residual stream of all tested models. Confirmed behaviourally at F1 42.1% and mechanistically at probe F1 0.43 in our experiments. RLHF does not fix it.
05

Our experiments

Four experiments were run on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct against CLadder, using an RTX 5080 in 4-bit QLoRA. The experiments were designed to probe internal geometry, not to produce a state-of-the-art model. Baseline accuracy near chance (53.5% vs 50% random) is expected and intentional.

Accuracy by rung

Fixed evaluation with corrected yes/no prompting. SFT on 2,000 CLadder examples produced a marginal overall gain, but the rung breakdown tells a different story.

ATE (average treatment effect), the core Rung 2 interventional quantity, dropped from 68.8% to 45.8%: a 23-point regression. SFT specifically disrupts interventional reasoning, consistent with the RLVR literature showing that SFT gains are rung-specific and unreliable for do() queries.

Probing study

For each of the six CausalCoT subskills, we extracted residual stream activations at all 28 layers from both Qwen2.5-7B and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, then trained logistic regression probes asking whether the subskill being exercised is linearly readable from the activations.

S1   graph extraction 0.964 0.971
S2   query classification 0.921 0.935
S3   formalization 0.416 0.431
S4   data collection 0.901 0.908
S5   estimand derivation 0.877 0.889
S6   arithmetic 0.944 0.951

Five of six subskills are strongly linearly readable, with F1 between 0.877 and 0.964. S3 sits at 0.416. The information needed to formally translate a causal query into symbolic notation is not robustly encoded as a linear direction in the residual stream. RLHF adds only +0.015 to S3 F1. Instruct training barely moves the needle. This is structural, and outcome-level training cannot fix it.

Difficulty breakdown

SFT helps on easy queries (short causal chains, direct associations) and hurts on hard ones (multi-step counterfactuals, interventional chains with confounders). The gain-regression split tracks rung difficulty closely, consistent with the accuracy results above.

We replicated the original CLadder prompting comparison: vanilla versus generic chain-of-thought versus explicit six-step CausalCoT, on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct. In 2023, GPT-4 with CausalCoT gained 8 points over vanilla. The 2026 result is a reversal.

Generic CoT now beats explicit CausalCoT. Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct has already internalised Pearl's reasoning structure through RLHF; the explicit six-step scaffold is redundant. Correlation queries show the biggest gain from CausalCoT, from 33.3% to 70.6%, the query type where S3 formalization is most load-bearing.

06

Mechanistic account

The following chain explains why the robustness gap persists across all post-training methods, and connects the behavioural evidence to the internal geometry of the model.

01
S3 formalization is weakly encoded in the residual stream (F1 0.43).
02
RLHF does not fix this. The delta is only +0.015.
03
When a perturbed query arrives, formalization fails first because it was never robustly encoded.
04
Downstream steps fail in sequence: classification, estimand derivation, arithmetic.
05
The robustness gap persists. The Rung 3 abduction deficit persists.

This is the first mechanistic account of why the robustness gap is universal. Previous work documented it behaviourally, reporting 30 to 62 point drops across models. We show where in the network it originates.

The connection to prior NeurIPS work: in persona representations, linear probes can read persona information from the residual stream with high F1, but activation steering cannot inject it back. MLP sublayers cancel the injected direction. This is the first confirmation that the same read/write asymmetry applies to skill representations, not just trait representations. It appears to be a general property of how transformer residual streams are organised.

07

Research proposal

The robustness gap is a representational problem, not a data or reward problem. Fixing it requires targeting the geometry of formalization. No existing method does this. The geometry-constrained fine-tuning approach adds an auxiliary loss to QLoRA training that explicitly rewards the residual stream for encoding the formalization subspace as a linearly separable direction.

In a small pilot, pure SFT degraded S3 probe F1 by 0.049. Training on correct answers makes the internal representation of formalization worse, because SFT teaches output imitation without encoding intermediate structure. The auxiliary separability loss recovered 35% of that degradation, bringing the drop from 0.049 to 0.032. The open question is whether this holds at scale, and whether the geometric improvement transfers to robustness gains on perturbed CLadder and real-world CauSciBench estimation.

Four concrete interventions follow from the analysis above, in order of priority.

Perturbation-augmented GRPO
Include variable-renamed and paraphrased CLadder and Corr2Cause examples in the RL training distribution. This forces the reward signal to be sensitive to variable-invariant reasoning rather than surface patterns. It requires minimal methodological novelty beyond data augmentation and directly targets the robustness gap.
Step-level process reward for CausalCoT
Verifiable steps can be checked automatically: graph extraction against the ground-truth DAG, query classification against the oracle label, arithmetic numerically. Formalization and estimand derivation can be verified using the same symbolic CI engine that generated CLadder's oracle answers. This is Math-Shepherd applied to the causal domain, providing dense training signal exactly where behavioural failures concentrate.
Rung-stratified curriculum RL
Begin GRPO training on Rung 1, progressively introduce Rung 2 and 3 as accuracy improves, using SCM-GRPO reward to prevent logic violations throughout. This directly targets the abduction deficit by ensuring the model builds a stable interventional foundation before attempting counterfactual reasoning.
Geometry-constrained auxiliary loss
The approach described above. The only intervention that addresses the mechanism rather than the symptom, targeting the representational geometry of S3 formalization directly. The next step is scaling to larger models and combining with the GRPO reward signal to test whether fixing the geometric bottleneck transfers to practical causal analysis.
