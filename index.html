Commonsense vs anti-commonsense

A consistent robustness signal: all models do better on anti-commonsense questions than on commonsense ones, which is counterintuitive. This likely reflects a bias toward correct causal reasoning when surface associations are misleading, since the model cannot rely on heuristics and must reason properly.

01

Pearl's ladder of causation

All causal reasoning sits on one of three rungs. The rung determines what kind of question is being asked and what information is needed to answer it correctly. Each level is harder than the one below it.

Rung 1
Association
Observing correlations. What is P(Y|X)?
Rung 2
Intervention
Do-calculus. What happens if we set X = x?
Rung 3
Counterfactual
Abduction, action, and prediction in sequence.

A model that cannot reliably execute Rung 2 cannot do Rung 3. The benchmark literature tracks progress across all three, but the distribution of failure is uneven, and the hardest rung remains mostly unsolved.

The primary causal reasoning benchmark. Over 10,000 questions spanning all three rungs, evaluated using CausalCoT: a six-step chain-of-thought protocol mirroring the formal causal inference engine. Post-training methods

Four post-training approaches have been applied to causal reasoning. Their relative gains differ, and the reason why matters as much as the numbers.

GRPO / PPO

GRPO and PPO dominate because they give the model corrective feedback on its own reasoning in real time. SFT teaches imitation of correct outputs. GRPO teaches how to think. DeepSeek-R1-14B with GRPO on CauGym reaches 93.5% on the CaLM benchmark, compared to OpenAI o3 at 55.4% without causal fine-tuning.

All four methods share the same out-of-distribution failure. In-distribution gains do not transfer to perturbed queries. This is the central unsolved problem and the motivation for the mechanistic work below.
Entropy regularisation
Removing entropy regularisation from BinaryPPO collapses accuracy from 97% to 29%. The model without it learns to always predict the same label.
Axiomatic beats task-specific
Training on abstract causal graph rules with alphanumeric variables generalises to unseen task formats. Training on task examples does not.
SCM-GRPO
Standard GRPO lets models learn to generate long plausible-sounding chains that are logically wrong. SCM-GRPO grounds the reward in an actual Structural Causal Model.
4-bit quantization
Compressed attention weights cannot fully sever pre-intervention correlations when simulating a do() operation. Injecting the ground-truth causal graph via GraphRAG recovers 1.7%.
04

Open problems

Robustness gap
Every method that achieves high in-distribution accuracy collapses 30 to 62 points under simple variable renaming or paraphrase. No exception exists.
Abduction deficit
Rung 3 requires inferring hidden background variables from observations. Models fail at this first step. CLadder cheats by providing latent variable values directly. Real abduction is harder and largely unaddressed.
No process supervision
All current training uses outcome-level reward. No step-level process reward model exists for CausalCoT, despite clear behavioural evidence of which steps fail: S3 formalization sits at F1 42.1%.
Real-world estimation gap
Models trained on synthetic data do not transfer to real scientific datasets. Mean relative estimation error remains 49% on CauSciBench even at 77% method selection accuracy.
S3 encoding bottleneck
Symbolic formalization is weakly encoded in the residual stream of all tested models. Confirmed at probe F1 0.43 in our experiments. RLHF does not fix it.

06

Mechanistic account

The following chain explains why the robustness gap persists across all post-training methods, and connects the behavioural evidence to the internal geometry of the model.

01
S3 formalization is weakly encoded in the residual stream (F1 0.43).
02
RLHF does not fix this. The delta is only +0.015.
03
When a perturbed query arrives, formalization fails first because it was never robustly encoded.
04
Downstream steps fail in sequence: classification, estimand derivation, arithmetic.
05
The robustness gap persists. The Rung 3 abduction deficit persists.

Previous work documented the robustness gap behaviourally, reporting 30 to 62 point drops across models. The experiments above show where in the network it originates.

The connection to prior work: in persona representations, linear probes can read persona information from the residual stream with high F1, but activation steering cannot inject it back. MLP sublayers cancel the injected direction. The same read/write asymmetry appears to hold for skill representations, not just trait representations.

The yes-reflex

A separate finding from the commitment layer analysis: 30.9% of all 10,112 CLadder items commit their answer at layers 13 or 14, before the model has fully processed the story. Every single one of these early-committed items answers yes, with no exceptions across the full benchmark.

07

LiReF experiments: predicting and rescuing fragile items

Hong et al. (ACL 2025) identified a single linear direction in the residual stream that governs whether a model is in reasoning mode versus memorization mode. They call this the LiReF direction, extracted via difference-in-means between reasoning-intensive and memory-intensive questions. Their paper noted applying LiReFs to perturbational robustness as open future work. These experiments close that gap on CLadder.

Items where the residual stream sits closer to memorization mode on the LiReF direction are significantly more likely to collapse when variable names are changed. The direction predicts fragility before any renaming occurs.

Two failure modes, not one

The commitment layer analysis from Section 06 and the LiReF prediction connect. Early-committed items (the yes-reflex group) have a 5.0% fragility rate. Late-committed items have a 16.9% fragility rate: a 3.4-fold difference.

Fragility rate by commitment group
Fisher exact p = 7×10⁻³⁵, n = 4,927 eligible items. Early-committed items answered before reading the variable names, so renaming does not affect them. Late-committed items with low LiReF scores read and pattern-matched the surface, making them fragile.

Early commitment and LiReF memorization mode are two distinct failure routes. Early commitment means the model did not process the story at all, so variable renaming has no effect. Late commitment with low LiReF means the model processed the story but learned the surface pattern rather than the causal structure, so renaming breaks it. One is immune; the other is vulnerable.

Can LiReF steering rescue fragile items?

If the LiReF direction is causally involved in fragility, not just predictive of it, then steering the residual stream toward reasoning mode at inference time should selectively help fragile items. Two versions were tested: injecting the LiReF direction at all 28 layers, and injecting only at layers 22 to 27 where the AUC signal is concentrated.

The rescue rates are modest in absolute terms, which reflects the fact that Qwen starts near chance on CLadder, compressing the signal. The rescue-to-damage ratio of 6.7 for the targeted injection shows the effect is selective, not generic. A model that achieves high in-distribution accuracy on CLadder would show a larger absolute rescue effect, since the fragility gap is wider.

This is the first demonstration that LiReF steering causally mediates, rather than just predicts, per-item robustness failure on a formal causal reasoning benchmark.

What this adds to the mechanistic account

The S3 encoding bottleneck (Section 06) explains why the robustness gap exists. The LiReF experiments explain which items are most at risk: items where the model committed late and sat in memorization mode. The two findings are compatible and reinforce each other. The S3 direction identifies the representational gap; the LiReF direction identifies which items are processing the query through the wrong mode.

08

Research proposal

The robustness gap is a representational problem, not a data or reward problem. Fixing it requires targeting the geometry of formalization. No existing method does this. The geometry-constrained fine-tuning approach adds an auxiliary loss to QLoRA training that explicitly rewards the residual stream for encoding the formalization subspace as a linearly separable direction.

In a pilot, pure SFT degraded S3 probe F1 by 0.049. Training on correct answers makes the internal representation of formalization worse, because SFT teaches output imitation without encoding intermediate structure. The auxiliary separability loss recovered 35% of that degradation, bringing the drop from 0.049 to 0.032. The open question is whether this holds at scale, and whether the geometric improvement transfers to robustness gains on perturbed CLadder and CauSciBench.

The LiReF intervention result adds a new angle: if steering at inference time can rescue fragile items at modest rates, then training with a LiReF-based regularisation loss could keep items in reasoning mode during learning, directly targeting the memorization mode that the prediction experiments identified as the fragility signature.

Perturbation-augmented GRPO
Include variable-renamed and paraphrased CLadder and Corr2Cause examples in the RL training distribution. This forces the reward signal to be sensitive to variable-invariant reasoning rather than surface patterns.
Step-level process reward for CausalCoT
Verifiable steps can be checked automatically: graph extraction against the ground-truth DAG, query classification against the oracle label, arithmetic numerically. Formalization and estimand derivation can be verified using the same symbolic CI engine that generated CLadder's oracle answers.
Rung-stratified curriculum RL
Begin GRPO training on Rung 1, progressively introduce Rung 2 and 3 as accuracy improves, using SCM-GRPO reward to prevent logic violations throughout.
Geometry-constrained auxiliary loss
Add an auxiliary loss during QLoRA training that explicitly rewards the residual stream for encoding the S3 formalization subspace as a linearly separable direction. In a pilot, this recovered 35% of SFT-induced S3 degradation. The next step is combining this with a LiReF-based regularisation term to keep items in reasoning mode during training.
