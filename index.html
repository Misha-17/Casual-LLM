Claude Fable 5 on CauSciBench: method selection is decent, effect estimates are not
We ran Claude Fable 5 through our causal agent (CAIS) on all three CauSciBench splits: real research data, QRData-CI, and synthetic data. Across 357 tasks, Fable 5 picks a reasonable statistical method about 64% of the time. But once you check whether the actual numeric estimate is close to the true answer, that score drops to 29%. The gap is biggest on real research data, where messy, high-cardinality variables trip up the estimation step even when the method choice is right.

CauSciBench: Claude Fable 5 results

CauSciBench gives a model a research question and a real dataset, and asks it to pick a causal inference method, run it, and interpret the result. Method accuracy: how often the chosen method matches the reference method. Effect accuracy: a partial-credit score based on how close the numeric estimate is to the true answer, counted only when the method was also correct. Coverage: how often a usable numeric estimate was produced at all.
Method selection holds up reasonably well across all three splits, staying above a third even on the hardest split. Effect accuracy is a different story: it falls hardest on real data, where the model has to deal with messy real-world variables instead of clean synthetic ones.

What this means

Method vs. execution
Fable 5 is noticeably better at naming the right method than at actually running it and getting a correct number. This gap is largest on real data, where it drops from 65.1% method accuracy to just 18.1% effect accuracy.
Synthetic is the easy case
Synthetic data gives the best results across the board, since the underlying relationships are simpler and the variables are clean. This gap between synthetic and real performance is a known pattern in causal reasoning benchmarks.
QR is hardest for method choice
QRData-CI has the lowest method accuracy of the three splits (35.9%), suggesting its quantitative reasoning questions are harder to map onto a standard causal method than the other two splits.
Note on evaluation: effect accuracy and coverage are computed only for rows where the model's answer could be automatically matched back to its source question. A portion of rows, especially on the real split, could not be matched this way and are counted as incorrect. Actual performance is likely somewhat higher than shown here.

Failure pattern analysis

We checked reasoning traces of wrong answers for three recurring patterns: the final answer contradicting a correct reasoning trace ("answer flip"), a correct causal graph with the wrong sign on the effect ("direction error"), and confusing collider conditioning with confounder adjustment on backdoor items ("backadj confusion"). GPT-5.4 Nano shows none of these patterns simply because it produces no reasoning trace to check.

Failure pattern counts, wrong answers with reasoning traces
Gemini 2.5 Flash flips a correct trace into a wrong final answer in 18.2% of its wrong answers (265 of 1,456); Claude Sonnet 4.6 flips in 12.9% (115 of 892). Sonnet shows more direction errors (163 vs 12); Gemini shows more backadj confusion (178 vs 34).

Forced thinking experiment

Claude Opus 4.8 skipped extended thinking on 4,608 of 10,105 items when reasoning was left optional. Rerunning those items with a forced minimum thinking budget of 1,024 tokens raised accuracy on that subset from 88.60% to 90.23%, a gain of 1.63 points. Of the items that changed, 114 flipped from wrong to correct and 39 flipped from correct to wrong, a rescue-to-break ratio of roughly 2.9 to 1.

Accuracy change from forced thinking, by query type (Opus 4.8)
Hardest query types gain the most: correlation +6.1pp, nde +3.4pp, nie +3.0pp. The two easiest query types, marginal and collider_bias, see small regressions from forced thinking, consistent with extra deliberation occasionally talking the model out of an already-correct answer.
Reasoning is the gap
GPT-5.4 Nano, run without extended thinking, scores 54.80%, about 40 points below every reasoning-enabled model and barely above chance. Model tier matters far less than whether reasoning was switched on.
GPT-5.5 leads, narrowly
GPT-5.5 tops the field at 95.09% with high reasoning effort, 1.83 points ahead of Grok 4.20 and 3.70 points ahead of Claude Opus 4.8. The top three are frontier reasoning models within 4 points of each other.
Sonnet's Rung 3 strength
Claude Sonnet 4.6 posts 91.16% on Rung 3 (counterfactual), the best counterfactual score of any model on the leaderboard, and well ahead of its own Rung 2 score (84.78%), its weakest rung.
Forced thinking rescues Opus
Forcing a minimum thinking budget on Opus 4.8's null-reasoning items raised accuracy from 88.60% to 90.23% (+1.63pp), rescuing 114 items for every 39 it broke. The hardest query type, correlation, gained the most (+6.1pp).

Background

Pearl's ladder of causation

All causal reasoning sits on one of three rungs. The rung determines what kind of question is being asked and what information is needed to answer it correctly. Each level is harder than the one below it.

Rung 1
Association
Observing correlations. What is P(Y|X)?
Rung 2
Intervention
Do-calculus. What happens if we set X = x?
Rung 3
Counterfactual
Abduction, action, and prediction in sequence.

A model that cannot reliably execute Rung 2 cannot do Rung 3. The benchmark literature tracks progress across all three, but the distribution of failure is uneven, and the hardest rung remains mostly unsolved.

Benchmark landscape

CLadder (NeurIPS 2023)

The primary causal reasoning benchmark. Over 10,000 questions spanning all three rungs, evaluated using CausalCoT: a six-step chain-of-thought protocol mirroring the formal causal inference engine. The six steps are graph extraction, query classification, data collection, symbolic formalization, estimand derivation, and arithmetic.

Post-training methods

Four post-training approaches have been applied to causal reasoning. Their relative gains differ, and the reason why matters as much as the numbers.

GRPO / PPO

GRPO and PPO dominate because they give the model corrective feedback on its own reasoning in real time. SFT teaches imitation of correct outputs. GRPO teaches how to think. DeepSeek-R1-14B with GRPO on CauGym reaches 93.5% on the CaLM benchmark, compared to OpenAI o3 at 55.4% without causal fine-tuning.

All four methods share the same out-of-distribution failure. In-distribution gains do not transfer to perturbed queries. This remains the central unsolved problem in the field.
Entropy regularisation
Removing entropy regularisation from BinaryPPO collapses accuracy from 97% to 29%. The model without it learns to always predict the same label.
Axiomatic beats task-specific
Training on abstract causal graph rules with alphanumeric variables generalises to unseen task formats. Training on task examples does not.
SCM-GRPO
Standard GRPO lets models learn to generate long plausible-sounding chains that are logically wrong. SCM-GRPO grounds the reward in an actual structural causal model.
4-bit quantization
Compressed attention weights cannot fully sever pre-intervention correlations when simulating a do() operation. Injecting the ground-truth causal graph via GraphRAG recovers 1.7%.
Background

Open problems

Robustness gap
Every method that achieves high in-distribution accuracy collapses 30 to 62 points under simple variable renaming or paraphrase. No exception exists.
Abduction deficit
Rung 3 requires inferring hidden background variables from observations. Models fail at this first step. CLadder provides latent variable values directly rather than requiring real abduction, which is harder and largely unaddressed.
No process supervision
All current training uses outcome-level reward. No step-level process reward model exists for CausalCoT, despite clear behavioural evidence of which steps fail most often.
Real-world estimation gap
Models trained on synthetic data do not transfer well to real scientific datasets. Mean relative estimation error remains around 49% on CauSciBench even at high method selection accuracy, a pattern our own Fable 5 results reproduce.
